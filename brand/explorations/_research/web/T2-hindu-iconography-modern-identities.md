# T2 — How modern Hindu/dharmic organizations handle sacred iconography in their marks: nobody draws the lingam, and sanctity lives in placement

*Research track T2 · web survey · compiled 2026-06-07.*
*Scope: how named Hindu/dharmic institutions (Isha, ISKCON, Art of Living, BAPS, Chinmaya Mission, Ramakrishna Mission, and the Shaiva Jyotirlinga temple trusts Somnath / Kashi Vishwanath / Mahakaleshwar / TTD) treat deity imagery, lingam, lotus, chakra, om and kalasha in their identity marks; plus the merchandise sanctity-in-use norms and controversies (gods/om on footwear, below-waist garments, doormats) that bind any brand whose mark may encode a shivling. Sources dated inline; logos that I retrieved and inspected as images are marked **[image-verified]**. Where a current-state claim could not be confirmed from a primary page it is flagged.*

---

## Findings

### F1 — The deity-imagery spectrum: the bigger and more national the dharmic brand, the more it abstracts AWAY from a literal deity or lingam
Across the named organizations the identity *mark* (as distinct from a statue or campaign symbol) is almost never a worship-grade deity image (murti) and *never* a literal Shiva lingam:
- **Isha Foundation** — **[image-verified, isha_logo_black.svg]** the actual brand logo is a **calligraphic lowercase "isha" wordmark** with a single **saffron dot over the "i."** No deity face, no lingam — structurally the same wordmark-plus-colored-dot device as RTAM's bindu. (The famous **Adiyogi** Shiva *bust* — framed as "a symbol… a simple sign that points people to yoga," not a worship-form — is a separate 112-ft statue/campaign symbol, **not** the organization's logo; it is figurative deity-*as-sign*, and even it depicts a face, never a lingam.) (Isha logo asset — https://static.sadhguru.org/d/46272/1690792477-isha_logo_black.svg ; Adiyogi context — https://www.facebook.com/ishafoundation/posts/10159351167654947/ , https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiyogi_Shiva_bust )
- **ISKCON** — a lotus device + lettering, with the four symbols of Vishnu (lotus, conch, disc/chakra, club) at the corners; the running mark is the lotus, not Krishna's image. (ISKCON Communications, official logo — https://www.iskconcommunications.org/resources/official-iskcon-logo )
- **Art of Living** — a rising sun + two swans, where the swan forms are stylized so the pair also reads as a lotus ("swan-as-lotus"); pure abstraction of vahana + element, no deity. (Sri Sri Ravi Shankar on the logo — http://wisdomfromsrisriravishankar.blogspot.com/2019/05/the-meaning-of-rising-sun-and-two-swans.html )
- **Chinmaya Mission** — the lamp of knowledge crowned by the **hamsa** (swan of discrimination) + Chinmayananda's OM signature; an emblem of *concepts*, no deity figure. (Chinmaya Mission emblem — https://www.chinmayamission.com/mission-right-first/ ; Sandeepany symbolism — https://www.chinmayamissionmumbai.com/chinmaya_pradeep/65-exhibits/26-symbolism-of-the-sandeepany-logo )
- **Ramakrishna Mission / Belur Math** — Vivekananda's own 1901 emblem: wavy **water** (karma), **lotus** (bhakti), rising **sun** (jnana), encircling **serpent** (yoga/kundalini), and a **swan** (Paramatman). A pure cosmogram synthesizing the four yogas — a deity-centric order chose an *abstract diagram of principles*, not Ramakrishna's image. (Belur Math, official Emblem page — https://belurmath.org/emblem/ )

### F2 — The Shaiva Jyotirlinga temple trusts — the orgs most entitled to draw a lingam — do not. This is the load-bearing precedent for RTAM. **[image-verified]**
The three Shiva Jyotirlinga trusts named in the brief each guard a literal shivling, yet none puts a lingam in its mark:
- **Shree Somnath Trust** (first Jyotirlinga) — **[image-verified, /static/img/Somnath_logo.png]** an abstract **trident-flame** (the flame doubles as a trishul, read as the union of Shiva and Shakti) inside a **deep cosmic-blue circle**, wordmark SOMNATH, tagline *"Experience eternal energy."* No lingam, no deity face. Per the trust's stated new visual identity, the flame is "the union of Shiva and Shakti, creation, and cosmic balance" and the blue circle is the *Anant Urja Kshetra* — "infinite energy and the cosmic womb." This is a sacred symbol abstracted into a **circle-bounded cosmogram** — structurally the same move RTAM is contemplating for the ṛta wheel. (Somnath Trust — https://somnath.org/ ; logo asset https://somnath.org/static/img/Somnath_logo.png )
- **Shri Kashi Vishwanath Temple Trust** — **[image-verified, /frontend/images/logo.png]** a stylized **temple-spire (shikhara) silhouette** with flag, in a saffron watercolor splash, beside the wordmark. The deity inside is a Jyotirlinga; the mark depicts the *architecture*, not the lingam (whether by deliberate refusal or simply by choosing the building as the recognizable landmark, the effect is the same — no lingam in the mark). (https://shrikashivishwanath.org/ ; logo asset https://www.shrikashivishwanath.org/frontend/images/logo.png )
- **Mahakaleshwar (Ujjain)** — official channels exist (https://mahakaleshwar.net/ , Instagram @shrimahakaleshwarjyotirlingam); I could **not retrieve a clean logo asset to image-verify** its composition (the homepage path 404'd). Flagged as unconfirmed rather than guessed.
- **Tirumala Tirupati Devasthanams (TTD)** — Vaishnava, not Shaiva, but the same lesson: the identity anchor is the **tirunamam** (the abstract U-shaped namam with central line), a *symbol*, not Venkateswara's murti. (TTD news, *Tirunamam — the traditional symbol* — https://news.tirumala.org/tirunamam-the-traditional-symbol-in/ — page certificate-failed on fetch; cited from search index.)

**The tier split is itself the finding.** Community temples will put a stylized lingam in a busy composite — e.g. **Austin Hindu Temple** renders Mount Kailash + Shiva Lingam + lotus-in-water + AUM + shankha-chakra in one crowded emblem (https://www.austinhindutemple.org/logo/ ). But the **national-scale Shiva trusts abstract the lingam out entirely** (trident-flame, temple-spire). RTAM's stated ambition — Instagram / YouTube / web / print / merchandise at scale — places it in the *second* tier, where the precedent is unanimous: **abstract the sacred form; do not depict the lingam literally.**

### F3 — How the recurring elements get abstracted (lotus / chakra / om / kalasha / lingam)
A consistent grammar emerges from the marks above:
- **Lotus** → reduced to a few clean petals or a radial silhouette (ISKCON, RKM, Art of Living's swan-lotus). Never photorealistic.
- **Chakra / wheel** → a clean geometric ring/disc; meaning carried by spoke count, not ornament (the cosmic-circle ground in Somnath; the namam-as-symbol logic in TTD).
- **Om / AUM** → used as a *signature/wordmark element* (Chinmaya's OM, RKM lettering), almost never as the whole pictorial mark — and treated as the **most commodified** sacred symbol, so the most exposed on merchandise (see F4).
- **Kalasha / flame / trishul** → abstracted to a single clean silhouette; Somnath's flame *is* a trishul *is* Shiva-Shakti — one shape carrying layered meaning, the opposite of stacking separate icons.
- **Lingam** → **not depicted at all by national brands**; its presence is implied by an abstract container (a circle/cosmic-womb, a flame, a spire) rather than drawn. This is the single most relevant pattern for RTAM's "shivling as the hub of a wheel."

### F4 — Sanctity-in-use: the authoritative do-NOT-place list is about footwear, below-the-waist, and anything destroyed/stepped-on — and it binds *abstract symbols*, not only deity images
The Hindu American Foundation's *Guidelines for Commercial Use of Hindu Images* gives a concrete, quotable prohibition list. Hindu sacred images/symbols should **not** appear on: *"bathmats, toilet seats or seat covers; underwear (men's or women's), socks, pants or shorts; footwear, of any type and material; swimwear; door mats, rugs; alcohol, meat,"* plus yoga mats, skateboards, leather items, and "anything intended to be placed in spaces reserved for defecation, or placed on or near bodily organs of reproduction and elimination," or anything where the image "is likely to be destroyed through normal intended usage." Lower-risk, generally-acceptable surfaces: *"shirts, hats, pins, stickers; magnets; shawls; wall hangings, decorations, and decals; phone covers, laptop covers; tote bags."* (HAF — https://www.hinduamerican.org/projects/commercial-use-hindu-images-guide )

Crucially, **the HAF framework does not exempt abstract symbols.** Its own enforcement actions targeted the **Om** symbol — an abstract glyph, not a deity image — on **shoe insoles**: the objection was that "the feet, because they are in frequent contact with the ground, dirt, and dust, are considered unclean," so "if something unclean touches something that is sacred… it is seen as a sign of profound disrespect." (HAF open letter to Dollar Tree, sent 2015-06-26 — https://www.hinduamerican.org/blog/an-open-letter-to-dollar-tree-regarding-om-shoe-insoles/ — fetched via cache; the letter and date are confirmed.) The scholarly framing agrees the trigger is *treatment*, not the imprint itself: sacred symbols must be "kept apart from the realm of the mundane or the profane." (LSE Religion & Global Society, *Cultural appropriation: Hindu symbols within consumerism*, 2017 — https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/religionglobalsociety/2017/09/cultural-appropriation-analysing-the-use-of-hindu-symbols-within-consumerism/ )

### F5 — The controversy pattern is real, repeatable, and fast — boycotts + same-day removals; below-the-waist and feet are the bright lines
The merchandise-controversy record is consistent and well-documented: Amazon, Walmart, RageOn, Sears/Wayfair, and AliExpress have all been forced to pull deity/Om merchandise after Hindu protest, often within 24 hours, frequently with a `#BoycottAmazon`-style social cascade. The recurring objected-to formats are exactly the F4 list — **doormats, underwear, socks, footwear/flip-flops, swimwear, toilet-seat stickers, beer/alcohol.** The stated principle from the most prolific activist (Rajan Zed, Universal Society of Hinduism) is verbatim the placement rule: deities are "meant to be worshipped in temples or home shrines and not to wear on your hip/crotch or put your feet on or touch with your feet"; Goddess Durga "not to adorn one's buttocks and crotch" (re: swimwear). (Rajan Zed press archive — https://www.rajanzed.com/ ; The News Minute, *The American Hindu behind BoycottAmazon* — https://www.thenewsminute.com/news/american-hindu-behind-boycottamazon-rajan-zed-s-crusades-save-hinduism-44433 ; Retail Dive, *Sears, Wayfair under fire* — https://www.retaildive.com/news/sears-wayfair-under-fire-for-merchandise-decorated-with-hindu-imagery/432151/ ; The Commune, *13 Times E-Commerce Companies Disrespected Hindu Deities* — https://thecommunemag.com/13-times-e-commerce-companies-disrespected-hindu-deities-across-the-world ). The lesson for an *owner* of a sacred-encoded mark: the reputational blast radius is large and the bright lines (feet, below-waist, alcohol, leather, doormats, anything destroyed-in-use) are well-known and non-negotiable.

### F6 — The dot/bindu lineage is itself a sanctioned abstraction — RTAM's bindu is in good company
BAPS Swaminarayan's most-circulated mark element is the **tilak-chandlo** — a paste tilak with a central red dot (chandlo). The dot is explicitly devotional-abstract: it "is representative of the devotee" at the feet of the divine, tied to the ajna-chakra (third eye) as "the convergence of our existence in this physical world with our connection to the divine realm." A community of millions identifies via an abstract **dot**, not a deity image. (BAPS, *Tilak-Chandlo* — https://www.baps.org/Spiritual-Living/Hindu-Practices/Tilak-Chandlo-.aspx ; *The Red Dot: An Emblem of the Divine* — https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/red-dot-emblem-divine-bapsswaminarayansanstha ). This corroborates RTAM's existing strategy: an abstract sacred *point* is a legitimate, precedented dharmic identity device.

## Implications for RTAM

- **The Phase-2 "shivling as the hub of the ṛta wheel" must be an abstraction, never a depiction (F2, F3).** Every national-scale Shiva trust — including Somnath, custodian of the *first Jyotirlinga* — declines to draw the lingam, substituting an abstract container (cosmic circle, trident-flame, temple-spire). RTAM, which aspires to that scale, has unanimous precedent: encode the shivling as a **geometric hub form** (a disc, a seed-point, an *aniconic ovoid/dome implied by the wheel's center*), not as a recognizable lingam silhouette. Somnath's "flame-in-a-cosmic-circle = Shiva-Shakti" is the closest living model and should be cited in the cosmogram brief as the proof that a circle-bounded abstraction *is* the prestige move, not a compromise.

- **RTAM's bindu is already the right kind of move, and Isha is the direct mirror (F1, F6).** The gold bindu is exactly the **Isha "isha"-wordmark-plus-saffron-dot** / BAPS-chandlo / TTD-namam strategy: an abstract sacred point standing in for a consecrated form. Isha's brand mark in particular is structurally identical to RTAM's (calligraphic wordmark + one colored dot), which is strong validation. This is a strength to protect; the ṛta-wheel exploration should *grow from* the bindu (e.g. bindu = the wheel's hub = the aniconic shivling-point), not replace it with figuration.

- **The "merch at scale" ambition activates a whole sanctity-in-use regime RTAM has not yet written down (F4, F5).** The moment RTAM's mark encodes a shivling — even abstractly — placing that mark on the wrong product invites the exact boycott pattern that hit Amazon/Walmart. RTAM is the *brand owner*, so the risk is self-inflicted and fully controllable: it depends entirely on which products the foundation (and its licensees/print vendors) put the mark on. The bright lines are known and must become hard brand rules: **never footwear, never below-the-waist garments, never doormats/rugs, never alcohol or leather, never toilet/bath surfaces, never anything stepped-on or destroyed-in-use.** Acceptable merch surfaces (shirts/hats above the waist, tote bags, stickers, wall art, phone/laptop covers, vegetarian-food packaging) should be enumerated as the *allowed* set.

- **Abstraction lowers recognition risk but does NOT buy an exemption from placement rules (F4).** The Om-insole case proves an abstract glyph still triggered formal action. So even RTAM's deliberately-abstract shivling-hub gets no pass: the placement do/don'ts apply to the **whole mark** regardless of how legible the shivling is. The brief must not drift into "abstract = safe to put anywhere."

- **Layered-meaning-in-one-shape beats stacked icons (F3).** Somnath's single flame carrying trishul + Shiva-Shakti + cosmic-balance is the opposite of cramming separate lotus + lingam + 12 medallions + om into the hub. This reinforces (and is the Hindu-specific evidence for) the restraint already argued in T1's over-symbolism finding: RTAM's wheel should let *one* clean central form carry the shivling meaning, with the 12-medallion content living in the ring/seal, not a literal lingam icon at the center.

   > **§10 · Sacred-mark placement (sanctity in use)**
   > The RTAM mark encodes sacred content (the bindu; in Phase-2, an abstract shivling-hub). Treat it as a sacred symbol, not a generic logo.
   > **Never place the mark, seal, or any sacred-symbol element on:** footwear of any kind; socks; underwear, swimwear, or any below-the-waist garment; doormats, rugs, or floor surfaces; toilet, bath, or shower surfaces; alcohol or meat packaging; leather goods; yoga mats, skateboards, or anything stepped-on; anything likely to be destroyed, soiled, or worn out through normal use.

   > This applies to the **whole mark regardless of how abstract the shivling reads** — abstraction is not an exemption (cf. the Om-on-insoles case). Bind print vendors and licensees to this list in writing.
