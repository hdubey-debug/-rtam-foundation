# T4 — Trademark practicalities (India): wordmark vs device distinctiveness, religious-symbol registrability, the Ashoka-Chakra collision, and a clearance checklist for the finalist mark

## Findings

### F1 — The statute itself splits "can't distinguish" (9(1)) from "shouldn't be monopolised" (9(2)) — and BOTH bite a religious device, but only one bites a coined wordmark
Section 9, Trade Marks Act 1999 — verbatim:
- **9(1)(a)** refuses marks "which are devoid of any distinctive character, that is to say, not capable of distinguishing the goods or services of one person from those of another person";
- **9(1)(b)** refuses marks consisting exclusively of indications "which may serve in trade to designate the kind, quality, quantity, intended purpose, values, geographical origin or the time of production… or other characteristics of the goods or service";
- **9(1)(c)** refuses marks "which have become customary in the current language or in the bona fide and established practices of the trade";
- **Proviso to 9(1):** "a trade mark shall not be refused registration if before the date of application for registration it has acquired a distinctive character as a result of the use made of it or is a well-known trade mark."
- **9(2)** is an *absolute* bar with no acquired-distinctiveness escape: 9(2)(a) deceptive/confusing; **9(2)(b)** "it contains or comprises of any matter likely to hurt the religious susceptibilities of any class or section of the citizens of India"; 9(2)(c) scandalous/obscene; **9(2)(d)** "its use is prohibited under the Emblems and Names (Prevention of Improper Use) Act, 1950."
(Section 9 text: https://www.iplaw.in/section-9-absolute-grounds-for-refusal-of-registration/ ; corroborated https://thelegalschool.in/blog/section-9-of-trademark-act and https://tmwala.com/blog-section-9-of-the-trade-marks-act-1999/ . Official source https://www.indiacode.nic.in/show-data?actid=AC_CEN_11_60_00004_199947_1517807323972&sectionId=16794&sectionno=9&orderno=9 returned 403 to the fetcher — the wording above is from the iplaw.in mirror (fetched in full) and corroborated by matching search snippets from the two thelegalschool.in / tmwala.com sources.)
The structural point: a **coined/stylised wordmark** ("ṚTAM" set in Cinzel with a drawn bindu) is attacked only on the 9(1) distinctiveness axis, which the *proviso* lets you cure with use. A **religious device** (lotus / om / chakra / wheel) is attacked on **both** axes at once — weak under 9(1) AND potentially barred under 9(2)(b)/9(2)(d), where the proviso gives no relief. The two grounds are not interchangeable; 9(2) is the harder wall.

### F2 — Section 9(2)(d) is the bridge that pulls the Emblems Act into trademark refusal — and the Emblems Act *itself* independently blocks TM registration
Two interlocking statutes, not one:
1. **Trade Marks Act 9(2)(d)** bars registration of any mark whose use "is prohibited under the Emblems and Names (Prevention of Improper Use) Act, 1950" (F1).
2. **Emblems and Names Act 1950, s.3** independently prohibits trade/business/professional use of "any name or emblem specified in the Schedule… or any colourable imitation thereof" without prior Central Government permission; and the Act's registration clause directs that **no competent authority shall register a trade mark or design bearing any such emblem or name** if its use contravenes s.3. So the Emblems Act blocks the mark even before the TM Registrar reaches 9(2)(d). Contravention of s.3 carries a fine (up to ₹500 — nominal, but the registration bar is the real consequence). (Act + Schedule, official PDF https://www.indiacode.nic.in/bitstream/123456789/1896/1/aA1950-12.pdf returned 403 to the fetcher; Schedule read verbatim from https://www.advocatekhoj.com/library/bareacts/emblemsandnames/schedule.php and s.3/registration summary corroborated at https://indiankanoon.org/doc/126816437/ and https://tilakmarg.com/acts/emblems-and-names-prevention-of-improper-use-act-1950/ .)
The operative catch is **"or any colourable imitation thereof"** — the bar is not limited to a pixel-perfect copy of the protected emblem.

### F3 — The Emblems Act Schedule names "Ashoka Chakra"/"Dharma Chakra" by NAME and by the flag/seal representation — and specifies NO spoke count
Schedule entry 16 reads (verbatim from the bare-Act mirror): *"The names 'Ashoka Chakra' or 'Dharma Chakra' or the pictorial representation of Ashoka Chakra as used in the India[n] National Flag or in the official seal or emblem of the Government of India…"*. The Schedule's 21 entries protect, inter alia, the Indian National Flag (entry 3), the State Emblem/Government of India arms (entry 4), named freedom figures (entry 9-A: Gandhi, Nehru, Shivaji), and — relevant to a *Hindu* institution — the **Ramakrishna Math & Mission emblem (entry 18)** and **Sri Sarada Math emblem (entry 19)**. **No entry specifies a number of spokes.** (Schedule: https://www.advocatekhoj.com/library/bareacts/emblemsandnames/schedule.php .)
This is the single most important factual finding for Phase-2. The protected object is (a) the *words* "Ashoka Chakra"/"Dharma Chakra", and (b) the *specific 24-spoke wheel as it appears on the National Flag / State seal*. The real-world Ashoka Chakra on the flag has **24 spokes**; the temple cosmogram is **12-spoke**. The statute does not protect "any wheel"; it protects that named flag/seal device and its **colourable imitations**.

### F4 — Whether a 12-spoke dharmachakra is a "colourable imitation" of the 24-spoke Ashoka Chakra is a fact question — bounded, mitigable risk, not an automatic blocker
The chakra/dharmachakra is a **pan-Indic public-domain symbol** (Buddhist, Jain, Hindu; it predates and exists independently of the 1947 flag). The Emblems Act protects the *State's* version, not the religious motif at large. "Colourable imitation" is judged on resemblance likely to mislead into association with the official emblem; under the Act, a disputed "is it a colourable imitation?" question is referred to and finally decided by the **Central Government** (per the Act's competent-authority clause), so there is irreducible administrative discretion. Plain reading: a **12-spoke wheel, with non-flag styling (no navy-blue-on-white flag chromatics, no flag proportions, integrated into a lotus/temple cosmogram rather than presented as a free-standing state chakra)** is materially distinguishable from the 24-spoke flag device and is far less likely to be deemed a colourable imitation. Risk is **bounded and design-controllable** — primarily by spoke count (keep it 12, never 24), color (avoid the flag's chakra-navy on white), and context (embed it, don't isolate it). State it as a *managed* risk requiring agent sign-off, not a flat prohibition. (Basis: Schedule wording F3; "colourable imitation"/Central-Govt-reference clause, https://indiankanoon.org/doc/126816437/ and https://tilakmarg.com/acts/emblems-and-names-prevention-of-improper-use-act-1950/ .)

### F5 — Religious WORDS/NAMES and SYMBOLS sit in a crowded field: the Supreme Court refused "RAMAYAN" precisely because 20+ traders already used it and it had not acquired distinctiveness
In **Lal Babu Priyadarshi v. Amritpal Singh** (SC, 27 Oct 2015), the applicant sought "RAMAYAN" (with crown device) in Class 3 for incense/perfumery. The Court affirmed refusal, holding that **names of holy/religious books (Ramayan, Quran, Bible, Guru Granth Sahib, etc.) cannot be monopolised as trademarks**, and — critically for the crowded-field point — that the applicant "has not been able to establish that the word 'RAMAYAN'… had acquired a reputation… inasmuch as… there are **more than 20 traders in the city using the word 'RAMAYAN'** as a mark for similar products and also in different parts of the country." (https://www.casemine.com/judgement/in/58117ecc2713e179478bc83f ; https://indiankanoon.org/doc/53428453/ ; commentary https://www.indialaw.in/blog/intellectual-property-rights/lal-babu-priyadarshi-v-amritpal-singh-trademark-ramayan/ .) This is the mechanism the brief calls the "crowded-field problem": a common religious term/symbol (a) lacks inherent distinctiveness because millions share the spiritual referent, and (b) is already used by many traders, so it cannot point to one commercial source — and acquired-distinctiveness proof becomes near-impossible.

### F6 — IP-India's own Draft Manual maintains a published list of religious matter that hurts susceptibilities under 9(2)(b); deity names/symbols are treated as public-domain and rarely registrable
The IP-India **Draft Manual of Trade Marks Practice & Procedure** (official, ipindia.gov.in) lists, as matter likely to hurt religious susceptibilities under 9(2)(b), names/devices including **"Lord Buddha", "Shree Sai Baba", "Sri Ramakrishna", "Swami Vivekananda", "the Holy Mother / Sri Sarada Devi", "Balaji"**, and the emblems of the Ramakrishna Math & Mission. Indian commentary and case discussion (Attukal deity / "Sabarimala" matters) record the amicus/Manual position that **a deity's name cannot be monopolised because it is in the public domain**, while noting the courts apply 9(2)(b) inconsistently — mere use of a god/deity name is *not per se* offensive unless the goods/context are distasteful (e.g., a deity on liquor/footwear/tobacco). (Draft Manual referenced at https://ipindia.gov.in/writereaddata/Portal/IPOGuidelinesManuals/1_32_1_tmr-draft-manual.pdf — the fetcher got HTTP 404 on this exact path, so the list is cited from secondary sources that quote the Manual: https://www.khuranaandkhurana.com/2021/03/09/the-contours-of-religious-susceptibilities-for-trademarks-introduction ; https://www.mondaq.com/india/trademark/1728226/deity-names-being-trademarked-navigating-through-section-92b-and-judicial-inconsistencies ; https://www.legal500.com/developments/thought-leadership/registrability-of-religious-names-and-symbols-as-trademarks/ . **Flagged: Manual list not confirmed from the primary PDF this session.**) Practical takeaway for RTAM: the *temple's* deity-rooted name ("Rtambhareshvara"/shivling references) is more 9(2)(b)-exposed than the abstract coined wordmark.

### F7 — Device vs word: the registrable asset is the distinctive WHOLE; common religious elements inside a composite are weak and effectively unprotectable in isolation
The 2026 ICLG India report confirms the practice line: marks "devoid of distinctive character, such as descriptive or generic terms" are refused unless they acquire secondary meaning; word marks are filed in plain text, device/logo marks as a graphical reproduction; and an otherwise non-distinctive mark "may be registered if it has acquired distinctiveness through extensive use prior to the date of application." (https://iclg.com/practice-areas/trade-marks-laws-and-regulations/india , 2026.) The corollary, consistent with F5/F6: where a logo is a **composite** (e.g., a coined wordmark + a common religious device), the *protectable distinctiveness lives in the coined/stylised whole and the wordmark*, **not** in the generic lotus/chakra/om element — which a third party can freely reuse and which a Registrar may require to be disclaimed or treated as non-distinctive. Registering the composite does **not** give RTAM a monopoly on the lotus or the wheel.

### F8 — Relevant Nice classes for this program: 36 (donations/financial), 41 (education/cultural), 45 (religious & charitable social services) — file the wordmark across the actual activity set, per class
India follows the Nice Classification (45 classes; 35–45 are services). For a charitable foundation + temple:
- **Class 36** — "Insurance; financial affairs; monetary affairs; real estate affairs" — covers **charitable fundraising / collection of donations / financial sponsorship**. (https://www.registerkaro.in/post/trademark-class-36-financial-and-real-estate ; https://selvams.com/kb/in/trademarks/trademark-classification-in-india/ )
- **Class 41** — "Education; providing of training; entertainment; sporting and cultural activities" — covers temple/foundation **education, cultural and religious events, publishing, festivals**. (https://www.patronaccounting.com/blog/trademark-classes-india-all-45-nice-classification )
- **Class 45** — "Legal services; security services…; and personal and social services rendered by others to meet the needs of individuals" — the WIPO Nice scope of Class 45 explicitly includes **religious services** and **charitable social-work / personal-and-social services**; this is the natural home for *temple worship / religious & spiritual services*. (https://www.trademarksindia.net/trademark-classes/class-45-personal-and-social-services.php ; https://aipgconsulting.com/2025/01/08/understanding-class-45-of-the-indian-trademarks-act/ )
India is single-class-fee (filing is *per class*), so covering all three is three filings/fees. **Flagged:** exact Class-36-vs-45 boundary for "collection of charitable donations" should be confirmed against the live WIPO Nice alphabetical list / TMclass with the agent (donation collection is sometimes placed in 36, the charitable *social work* in 45).

### F9 — Current (2026) IP-India government filing fee for a wordmark/device application (Form TM-A), verified against the official fee page
Per the official IP-India "Forms and Official Fees" page (https://ipindia.gov.in/pages/trade-marks/learn/forms-and-official-fees), **Form TM-A**, fee **"for each class and for each mark"**:

- **All other applicants (companies, trusts/societies not qualifying for the concession, etc.):** ₹9,000 e-filing · ₹10,000 physical.
(Corroborated https://www.mondaq.com/india/trademark/1615488/trademark-registration-fees-in-india-structure-costs and https://ebizfiling.com/blog/ipindiaonline-trademark-registration-guide-2025/ .) **Flag:** whether RTAM's legal vehicle (charitable trust / Section 8 company / society) qualifies for the lower ₹4,500 concession (it hinges on Startup-India/MSME-Udyam status, which a temple trust typically lacks) must be checked — budget the **₹9,000/class** rate unless the concession is confirmed. ## Implications for RTAM

- **The wordmark is RTAM's registrable core; the Phase-2 cosmogram is the legally weakest direction.** (F1, F5, F7.) "ṚTAM" set in Cinzel with a drawn bindu is a coined/stylised whole attacked only on 9(1) distinctiveness — curable by the acquired-distinctiveness proviso through real use. A lotus/chakra/om/wheel **device** is hit on both 9(1) (crowded field, low inherent distinctiveness) and 9(2)(b)/9(2)(d) (no proviso relief). This is a direct legal argument *for* keeping the primary mark the wordmark and *against* promoting a symbol-dense cosmogram into the primary slot — and it converges with T1's restraint/over-symbolism finding from a completely independent (legal, not aesthetic) direction.

- **"ṛta / ऋत" carries a mild 9(1)(b)/(c) descriptiveness flag, but the stylised whole + bindu rescues it.** ऋत (cosmic order) is a common Sanskrit word, so a *plain-text* "RTAM" word mark has some descriptive/generic exposure (cf. RAMAYAN, F5). RTAM should file the **stylised wordmark (Cinzel + drawn bindu lockup)** as the device/logo mark — the stylisation and the proprietary bindu add distinctiveness the bare word lacks — and separately consider the plain word mark only if the agent thinks it clears. The bindu being a *drawn* element (not Unicode) is a genuine distinctiveness asset here, not just a typographic nicety.

- **The 12-spoke wheel is a managed, design-controllable risk — NOT an automatic Ashoka-Chakra blocker — but the design must actively avoid colourable imitation.** (F3, F4.) The Emblems Act protects the **named** "Ashoka/Dharma Chakra" and the **24-spoke flag/seal** device and its **colourable imitations** — it does not protect "any wheel," and the dharmachakra is public-domain pan-Indic iconography. Concrete guardrails for the cosmogram: **keep the spoke count at 12 and NEVER 24**; do **not** use the flag's navy-chakra-on-white chromatics or flag proportions; do **not** present the wheel as a free-standing state-style emblem (embed it in the lotus/temple cosmogram); and do **not** caption it "Ashoka Chakra" or "Dharma Chakra" anywhere in brand copy or alt-text (the *names* are independently protected by Schedule entry 16). With those, risk drops materially — but "colourable imitation" is ultimately a Central-Government/Registrar judgment call, so this needs Indian-TM-agent sign-off before the cosmogram is finalised, not after.

- **A composite logo gives RTAM no monopoly on the lotus, the wheel, or om — and may draw a disclaimer.** (F7.) Even if a cosmogram registers as part of a composite, the common religious elements inside it are non-distinctive and reusable by anyone; the Registrar may disclaim them. So the cosmogram buys far less legal protection than the wordmark for the same filing cost — another reason the wordmark is the asset to protect first.

- **Watch entries 18/19 (Ramakrishna Math/Mission, Sri Sarada Math emblems) and the Draft-Manual deity list.** (F3, F6.) As a Hindu institution, RTAM must ensure its seal/cosmogram does not resemble the Ramakrishna Math/Mission or Sri Sarada Math protected emblems, and that deity-rooted temple naming ("Rtambhareshvara" / shivling imagery) is not deployed on goods/contexts a community could read as distasteful (the 9(2)(b) trap is *context*, per F6). Abstract beats figurative here too.

- **File the wordmark in 36 + 41 + 45 before public launch.** (F8, F9.) The program ships on Instagram/YouTube/web/print *now*; first-to-use helps build the acquired-distinctiveness record the proviso rewards, and early filing reduces the chance a third party files a confusingly similar mark first. Budget ≈ ₹27,000 government fee (3 classes at the ₹9,000 rate) + agent fees; confirm the trust's eligibility for the ₹4,500 concession.
